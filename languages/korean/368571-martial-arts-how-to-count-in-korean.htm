Martial arts: How to count in Korean

  • Top Article
  • All 2 Articles

by Can Tran

Created on: May 31, 2007

Counting in Korean is pretty easy, especially when counting from one to ten. I didn't even know how to speak a lick of Korean until I took Tae Kwon Do at the age of nine. Basically, counting in Korean is pretty simplistic because in all Korean martial arts schools such as Tae Kwon Do, instructors all count in Korean. All given commands such as bowing, thank you, and such are in Korean.

Basics of counting Korean are usually just one to ten. Such as jumping jacks, push-ups, punches, and kicks.

- "Hana" which is one.

- "Dool" which is two.

- "Seat" which is three.

- "Naet" which is four.

- "Ta-seot" which is five.

- "Yeo-seot" which is six.

- "Il-gop" which is seven.

- "Yeo-dul" which is eight.

- "A-hop" which is nine.

- "Yeol" which is ten.

Like if you're doing ten punches, kicks, or jumping jacks, instructors will count: "hana, dool, saet, naet, ta-seot, yeo-seot, il-gop, yeo-dul, a-hop, yeol."

If you go over ten then it's:

- "Yeol-Hana" which is eleven.

- "Yeol-Dool" which is twelve.

- "Yeol-Saet" which is thirteen.

- "Yeol-Naet" whichis fourteen.

Yeol has been established as ten in Korean. Hana is one, Dool is two, Saet is three, and Naet is four. Meaning from numbers eleven to ninteen, you take "Yeol" and add "Hana" to "A-hop" depending on the number. If it's fifteen, you take Yeol and add Ta-Seot to the end. Then you have "yeol-ta-seot" which is fifteen.

When you reach twenty which is "Sumol," it's the same thing. Instead of Yeol, you put Sumol in the front. For twenty-one, you say "sumol-hana."

For thirty which is "suron," you go "suron-hana" for thirty-one.

For forty which is "mahon," you go "mahon-hana" for forty-one.

For fifty which is "schwin," you go "schwin-hana" for fifty-one.

Counting in Korean is pretty simplistic. As long as you known the single figure numbers and the tens numbers, you should be fine.
